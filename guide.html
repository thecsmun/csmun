Guide to MUN

Everything you need to know about Model United Nations — from first-timer to seasoned delegate

THE BASICS

What is Model United Nations?

Model United Nations (MUN) is an academic simulation of the United Nations where students step into the shoes of diplomats, delegates, and world leaders to debate pressing global issues. Participants represent different countries or roles within committees, working together to draft resolutions, negotiate compromises, and solve real-world problems — just like the real UN.

CSMUN 2026 brings together young leaders from across schools to experience the thrill of diplomacy, the art of negotiation, and the power of informed debate. Whether you are a first-time delegate or a seasoned MUN participant, CSMUN offers an enriching platform to develop public speaking, critical thinking, and leadership skills.

How MUN Works

From research to resolutions — the journey of a delegate at CSMUN 2026

📚 1. Research Phase

Before the conference, every delegate must research their assigned country or role and the committee agenda.

  • Country Profile: Understand your country's history, foreign policy, allies, and stance on the agenda.
  • Agenda Analysis: Study the background paper provided by your committee's Executive Board.
  • Position Paper (Optional): Write a brief document outlining your country's position, proposed solutions, and diplomatic approach.

🎤 2. Opening Speeches

The committee begins with each delegate delivering a formal speech stating their country's position.

  • Speeches are typically 60–90 seconds long.
  • State your country's stance, key concerns, and proposed solutions.
  • Use diplomatic language and reference your country's policies.
  • The General Speaker.s List (GSL) determines the order of speeches.

💬 3. Formal Debate

Structured discussion where delegates speak in order from the General Speaker.s List (GSL).

  • General Speaker's List (GSL): A running list of delegates who wish to speak.
  • Yields: After a speech, the speaker may yield to questions, yield to another delegate, or yield to the Chair.
  • Rights: Right to Reply (response to personal insult or misrepresentation; Chair's discretion), Right to Personal Privilege (personal discomfort — e.g., cannot hear, room too hot; interrupts only if audibility-related), and Right to Order (when the Chair has made a procedural error; interrupts the speaker)

🤝 4. Caucuses — Where Negotiations Happen

Caucuses are the heart of MUN — where delegates discuss, negotiate, and draft solutions.

  • Moderated Caucus: A formal discussion on a specific subtopic. Delegates raise their placards to be called on by the Chair. Time per speaker is limited (e.g., 45 seconds).
  • Unmoderated Caucus: An informal break where delegates move around, form blocs, negotiate, and draft resolutions together. This is where the real diplomacy happens.

📝 5. Resolution Drafting

The goal of every committee is to produce a resolution — a formal document proposing solutions to the agenda issue.

  • Resolutions have three parts: Header (committee, topic, sponsors), Preambulatory Clauses (background context), and Operative Clauses (proposed actions).
  • Each clause begins with a specific phrase (e.g., "Noting with concern...", "Calls upon...").
  • Resolutions require signatories (delegates who want to see it debated) and sponsors (delegates who wrote it).
  • Once submitted, the resolution is presented, debated, amended, and voted upon.

🗳️ 6. Voting

After debate, the committee votes on resolutions and amendments.

  • Procedural Vote: Votes on motions (e.g., to introduce a resolution). Each delegation has one vote.
  • Substantive Vote: Votes on the resolution itself. "For," "Against," or "Abstain."
  • Resolutions pass by a simple majority (more "For" than "Against"), unless specified otherwise in the rules.

🏆 7. Awards

Outstanding delegates are recognised for their contributions.

  • Best Delegate: Highest award — given to the delegate who excelled in debate, diplomacy, and resolution writing.
  • High Commendation: Awarded to delegates who demonstrated exceptional performance.
  • Special Mention: Recognises notable contributions in specific areas.

Our Committees — A Closer Look

Each committee at CSMUN 2026 offers a unique debating experience. Choose the one that matches your interests.

🛡️ UNSC — United Nations Security Council

Agenda: Deliberation on the Legal Status of Non-State Armed Groups and Their Impact on International Peace and Security

The Security Council is the most powerful UN organ, responsible for maintaining international peace. Delegates in UNSC deal with crises, sanctions, and peacekeeping operations. Expect fast-paced debate and high-stakes decision-making.

Best for: Experienced delegates who enjoy crisis management and strategic thinking.

⚖️ UNHRC — United Nations Human Rights Council

Agenda: Special Session: Addressing the escalating humanitarian crisis in the Middle East, with focus on protection of human rights at sea within the Strait of Hormuz

The Human Rights Council works to promote and protect fundamental freedoms worldwide. Delegates discuss humanitarian crises, rights violations, and international justice. A committee for those passionate about social justice and human dignity.

Best for: Delegates interested in humanitarian issues, international law, and ethical debate.

☮️ DISEC — Disarmament and International Security Committee

Agenda: Examining the Impact of Foreign Military Intervention and Proxy Warfare as its Complex, on International Peace and Security with light on repercussions faced in events from the recent past

The First Committee of the UN General Assembly deals with disarmament, arms control, and global security. This committee tackles cutting-edge issues like AI in warfare, cyber threats, and autonomous weapons systems.

Best for: Delegates interested in technology, security, and future-oriented policy.

🇮🇳 LOK SABHA — House of the People

Agenda: Deliberation upon the implementation of the Uniform Civil Code (UCC) and its implications on Federalism and Customary Laws across India.

India's lower house of Parliament, where Members of Parliament debate national legislation and policy. This committee offers a unique domestic politics experience with a focus on Indian governance, security, and reform.

Best for: Delegates interested in Indian politics, parliamentary procedure, and domestic policy.

📰 IP — International Press

Agenda: Reporting, Journalism, Photography, and Media Coverage

The International Press committee is unique — instead of debating, delegates act as journalists covering the conference. They write articles, take photographs, conduct interviews, and publish a daily newsletter covering all committees.

Best for: Delegates with a passion for writing, photography, journalism, and media.

🏏 BCCI — Board of Control for Cricket in India Committee

Agenda: Discussing the need for the formulation of a strict code of conduct regarding on-field altercations between players and the digital accountability of young cricketers.

A unique committee exploring the intersection of sports, business, and public policy. Delegates discuss the governance structure of the BCCI, player regulations, financial sustainability, and the board's role in Indian sports culture.

Best for: Delegates passionate about sports management, business, and entertainment governance.

MUN Glossary

Essential terms every delegate should know

📋 Key Terms

Point of Order

Used when a delegate believes the Chair has made a procedural error. Interrupts the speaker.

Point of Personal Privilege

Used when a delegate experiences personal discomfort (e.g., cannot hear, room too hot). Interrupts the speaker only if related to audibility.

Point of Parliamentary Inquiry

A question directed to the Chair about parliamentary procedure. Does not interrupt a speaker.

Right to Reply

A request to respond to a personal insult or misrepresentation. Granted at the Chair's discretion.

Motion for a Moderated Caucus

Proposes a formal discussion on a specific subtopic with time limits per speaker.

Motion for an Unmoderated Caucus

Proposes an informal break for delegates to move around, negotiate, and draft resolutions.

Motion to Introduce a Resolution

Proposes that a submitted resolution be formally presented to the committee for debate.

Motion to Vote

Proposes that the committee move to voting procedure on the floor.

Placard

A sign with your country/role name. Raised to indicate you wish to speak or vote.

Bloc

A group of delegates with similar interests who work together to draft resolutions.

How to Write a Position Paper

A strong position paper sets the stage for effective debate

📄 What is a Position Paper?

A position paper is a brief document (1–2 pages) submitted before the conference that outlines your country's stance on the agenda. It demonstrates your preparation and gives the Executive Board a preview of your arguments.

  • Header: Committee name, agenda topic, country/role, delegate name.
  • Introduction: Brief background of the issue and why it matters to your country.
  • Country Stance: Your country's official position, past actions, and relevant UN votes.
  • Proposed Solutions: 2–3 actionable recommendations your country supports.
  • Conclusion: A diplomatic closing statement reaffirming commitment to cooperation.

💡 Tip: Use formal diplomatic language. Avoid first-person ("I think") — speak as your country ("India believes...").

Sample Resolution Structure

Understand the anatomy of a formal UN resolution

📜 Resolution Format

A resolution is divided into three parts: the header, preambulatory clauses, and operative clauses.

Committee: United Nations Security Council

Topic: Addressing Emerging Threats to International Peace

Sponsors: France, Japan, Brazil

Signatories: Germany, India, Nigeria, Canada, Australia


The Security Council,


Guided by the principles of the Charter of the United Nations,

Noting with concern the increasing frequency of cyber attacks on critical infrastructure,

Reaffirming the inherent right to self-defence under Article 51,


1. Calls upon all member states to implement robust cybersecurity frameworks;

2. Requests the Secretary-General to establish a UN task force on emerging threats;

3. Encourages international cooperation in intelligence-sharing and threat assessment;

4. Decides to remain seized of the matter.

📌 Preambulatory clauses (background) are italicised and end with commas. Operative clauses (actions) are numbered and end with semicolons — except the last which ends with a full stop.

MUN Procedures — Deep Dive

Master the motions, points, and flow of committee session

📌 Points & Motions — When to Use Them

Knowing the difference between a Point and a Motion is critical to navigating committee.

Point of Order

Chair made a procedural error. Interrupts speaker. Must be raised immediately.

Point of Personal Privilege

Personal discomfort (heat, noise, cannot hear). Interrupts only if audibility-related.

Point of Parliamentary Inquiry

Question about procedure. Does NOT interrupt a speaker. Wait for appropriate time.

Right to Reply

Response to personal insult/misrepresentation. Chair's discretion. Use sparingly.

Motion for Moderated Caucus

Formal discussion on a subtopic. Specify: total time, per-speaker time, topic. Votes on it.

Motion for Unmoderated Caucus

Informal break to negotiate and draft. Specify total time. Always passes.

Motion to Introduce a Resolution

Presents a submitted resolution to the committee for formal debate.

Motion to Move to Voting

Ends debate on the current topic and moves to voting procedure.

🔄 Voting Procedure Step-by-Step

When the committee moves to voting, here is the exact sequence:

  • 1. Roll Call: Chair calls each delegation — answer "Present" or "Present and Voting."
  • 2. Procedural Vote: Vote on whether to introduce the resolution (simple majority).
  • 3. Placard Vote: Delegates raise placards for For, Against, or Abstain.
  • 4. Tally: The dais counts and announces the result.
  • 5. Outcome: If passed, the resolution is adopted. If failed, debate may continue.

⚠️ "Present and Voting" means you cannot abstain. "Present" gives you the option to abstain.

Common Mistakes — And How to Avoid Them

Learn from others' experience so you can hit the ground running

⚠️ Top 10 Delegate Mistakes

  • Not researching your country: You cannot debate effectively without knowing your country's position. Read the background paper and research your country's foreign policy.
  • Reading your speech: A speech read from paper lacks impact. Use bullet points and speak naturally. Maintain eye contact with the committee.
  • Speaking too much: Quality over quantity. One well-argued point is better than five shallow ones. Listen to others and respond thoughtfully.
  • Ignoring the directive: Your Executive Board gives guidance on the direction of debate. Follow their lead — they set the tone and pace.
  • Being too aggressive: Diplomacy is about persuasion, not confrontation. Respect opposing views. You can disagree without being disagreeable.
  • Not taking notes: Keep a running log of who said what, which blocs are forming, and key arguments. This helps you draft stronger resolutions.
  • Forgetting placard etiquette: Raise your placard straight up. Do not wave it. Wait to be called before speaking.
  • Missing unmoderated caucus: The real work happens in unmod. Use this time to negotiate, form blocs, and draft. Do not sit idle.
  • Writing weak clauses: Operative clauses must be specific, actionable, and realistic. Avoid vague language like "work together" or "consider."
  • Not submitting position paper (if applicable): If your committee requires a position paper, make sure to submit it on time — it may be considered for awards.

Everything you need to bring and remember

🎒 What to Bring

  • Delegate badge (provided at registration)
  • Printed position paper (2 copies)
  • Notebook and pen
  • Background paper (printed or on device)
  • Laptop/tablet for drafting (optional)
  • Water bottle
  • Formal attire (already dressed)

✅ Pre-Conference

  • Read the background paper thoroughly
  • Write and submit position paper
  • Research your country's foreign policy
  • Prepare a 90-second opening speech
  • Prepare 3–5 talking points
  • Know your allies and adversaries
  • Get a good night's sleep

🏛️ During Conference

  • Arrive 30 minutes before first session
  • Listen carefully to opening remarks
  • Take notes on other delegates' positions
  • Network during breaks and unmod
  • Participate in at least one moderated caucus
  • Co-sponsor or sign a resolution
  • Stay until the committee adjourns

Useful Phrases for Debate

Sound like a seasoned diplomat with these essential MUN phrases

🗣️ Opening Your Speech

"Honourable Chair and fellow delegates..."
"The delegation of [country] firmly believes that..."
"We must underscore the urgent need for..."

🤝 Agreeing / Supporting

"We echo the sentiments expressed by the delegate of..."
"My delegation fully endorses the proposal put forth by..."
"We find common ground with the position of..."

⚡ Disagreeing Diplomatically

"While we respect the delegate's perspective, we must respectfully disagree..."
"My delegation holds a divergent view on this matter..."
"We urge the committee to consider an alternative approach..."

❓ Asking Questions

"Would the delegate kindly elaborate on..."
"Is the delegate suggesting that..."
"What evidence does the delegation have to support..."

💡 Proposing Solutions

"[Country] proposes the establishment of a task force to..."
"We call upon the international community to..."
"A collaborative framework involving multiple stakeholders would..."

🎯 Closing Your Speech

"In conclusion, my delegation urges all members to..."
"We look forward to a productive and collaborative debate."
"I yield my time to the Chair / to questions / to the next speaker."

MUN Myths — DEBUNKED

Common misconceptions about Model United Nations

❌

MYTH: You need prior MUN experience

✅

TRUTH: 40% of CSMUN delegates are first-timers. We provide training sessions and mentorship.

❌

MYTH: You must be an extrovert

✅

TRUTH: MUN values thoughtful arguments over loud voices. Introverts often excel through research and diplomacy.

❌

MYTH: Only "smart" kids win awards

✅

TRUTH: Awards go to delegates who collaborate, negotiate, and contribute — not just those who speak the most.

❌

MYTH: MUN is just debates

✅

TRUTH: MUN is 30% debate, 70% negotiation, networking, and collaborative problem-solving.

❌

MYTH: You need perfect English

✅

TRUTH: Clarity > Fluency. Good ideas matter more than accent or vocabulary.
